Geographic Location & Time Period

The Irigwe inhabit the Jos Plateau of central Nigeria. Unique polyandrous customs persisted until the tribal council outlawed them in 1968.

Cultural Traditions & Beliefs

After marrying one husband, a woman could take secondary husbands (dịkpọ̀), each recognized by the community. Husbands shared resources and labor, while children’s paternity was socially assigned based on the wife’s residence.

Sociopolitical Structure

Polyandry created broad kin networks linking villages. Marital ties served as inter-clan shields during conflict and facilitated communal labor sharing on marginal farmland.

  • Resource Pooling: Multiple husbands contributed to harvests and livestock care.
  • Kinship Bonds: Marriages crossed clan lines, fostering solidarity.
  • Infertility Solution: Secondary husbands ensured childbearing without divorce.

Anthropological Analysis

Anthropologists describe Irigwe polyandry as adaptive: it prevented land fragmentation, spread economic risk, and upheld women’s autonomy. Its abolition reflected external pressures rather than internal social failings.

Legal & Modern Status

Nigerian civil law does not recognize polyandry. The practice survives informally among elders but carries no official status or inheritance rights.
