Time Period & Context

From c.2000–500 BCE, Mesopotamian and Canaanite elites practiced polygyny. Biblical patriarchs—Abraham, Jacob, David, Solomon—maintained multiple wives and concubines.

Cultural Traditions & Beliefs

Legal codes like Hammurabi’s allowed a second wife if the first was barren. Polygyny cemented tribal alliances and secured heirs in high-mortality times.

Sociopolitical Structure

Kings used marriage diplomacy—Solomon’s 700 wives and 300 concubines were political hostages. Concubines held lower status than wives but their children could inherit.

  • Alliance-Building: Marriages linked royal houses across city-states.
  • Succession Security: Multiple wives increased chances of male heirs.
  • Legal Flexibility: Concubinage allowed nobles to have non-official unions.

Anthropological Analysis

Scholars argue polygyny mitigated high infant mortality and reinforced hierarchical societies. Biblical stories (Sarah/Hagar, Leah/Rachel) underscore both cooperation and jealousy.

Legal & Later Developments

By the 1st century CE, rabbinic edicts began favoring monogamy. Early Christianity adopted monogamy under Roman influence.
