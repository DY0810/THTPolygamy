Historical Context

From ancient dynasties through the Qing era, China, Joseon Korea, and samurai Japan sanctioned one principal wife and multiple concubines, codified in imperial law.

Cultural Traditions & Beliefs

Confucian ideals elevated the primary wife (diqi) while concubines (qie) provided additional heirs. Sons by concubines were legitimate but lower in the succession order.

Sociopolitical Structure

Elite households maintained strict hierarchy among consorts. Wealthy men among the gentry took concubines to secure male-line continuity without diluting official lineage.

  • Lineage Maintenance: Concubines ensured heirs when the wife was barren.
  • Rank Distinctions: Rigid court protocols governed concubine roles.
  • Social Prestige: More concubines signaled greater status.

Anthropological Analysis

Historians observe that concubinage limited the legal complications of multiple official marriages while satisfying dynastic needs.

Legal & Modern Status

Abolished by Japan’s Meiji Civil Code (1898) and China’s 1950 Marriage Law, concubinage and polygyny are illegal today; any “mistresses” hold no legal standing.

Textual Evidence

“The diqi commands all; the concubine bows.”
— Qing Code of Imperial Household
