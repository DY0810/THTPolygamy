Historical Context

Polygyny among Mughal emperors and Rajput princes served diplomatic ends; fraternal polyandry in Himalayan Pahari groups preserved scarce farmland.

Cultural Traditions & Beliefs

Hindu epics like the Mahabharata describe Draupadi married to five brothers; certain Himalayan tribes claimed direct descent. Muslim personal law allowed up to four wives.

Sociopolitical Structure

Royal polygyny forged alliances between princely states. Polyandry prevented parceling of land among heirs. In Kerala, the Nair sambandham system permitted visiting unions under matrilineal customs.

  • Alliance Marriages: Rajput kings married princesses from rival clans.
  • Land Conservation: Himalayan brothers shared a wife to keep estates intact.
  • Sexual Customs: Nair women held multiple relationships without formal polygyny.

Anthropological Analysis

Ethnographers note that polyandry limited population pressure in marginal regions. Royal polygyny balanced between protocol and concubinage to avoid dynastic disputes.

Legal & Modern Status

The Hindu Marriage Act (1955) outlawed polygamy for Hindus. Muslim men still may marry up to four wives under personal law, subject to arbitration. Polyandry is illegal but persists clandestinely.

Textual Evidence

“These five brothers wed her by divine decree.”—Draupadi and the Pandavas (Mahabharata)
