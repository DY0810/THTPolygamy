Comparison of Polygamous Practices Across Regions
Region/Society Form & Prevalence (Historical) Key Drivers of Polygamy Legal Status (Today)
Yoruba (Nigeria) – Sub-Saharan Africa Polygyny (one man, multiple wives); historically prevalent with polygamy rates as high as 30–40% in some rural areas. Indicators of wealth, increased household labor (farming/trade), bridewealth, and social prestige. Cultural and religious acceptance (both indigenous and later Islamic influences) reinforce the practice. Legally recognized under customary or Islamic law in northern Nigeria; civil law generally upholds monogamy in southern regions. Overall, the practice is declining in urban areas.
Maasai (Kenya/Tanzania) – East Africa Polygyny; an ideal in pastoral communities with multiple wives per elder man. Pastoral economy (more wives enable greater cattle management), social organization (age-set systems), and cultural idealization of elder male status. Legally recognized under customary law in both Kenya and Tanzania. Modern trends indicate a gradual shift towards monogamy in urban areas.
Zulu (South Africa) – Southern Africa Polygyny traditionally practiced among chiefs and elites; historically integrated into chiefdom and political alliances. Use of lobola (bridewealth in cattle), alliance formation, and a cultural imperative for heirs. Social status and the reinforcement of patriarchal authority are central. Legally recognized under the Recognition of Customary Marriages Act; balanced by constitutional promotion of gender equality. Customary practice remains among traditionalists.
Irigwe (Nigeria) – Unique Case in Africa Polyandry (one woman, multiple husbands); a rare historical practice. Intended to forge inter-village bonds, pool husbands’ resources, and address issues such as infertility in marginal farming contexts. Officially outlawed by the tribe in 1968 and not recognized by modern Nigerian law; largely assimilated into monogamous norms.
Middle East (Arab & Islamic) Predominantly polygyny (with a maximum of four wives as prescribed by Islamic law); historically practiced by elites in harems. Religious sanction (Quran 4:3), care for widows/orphans, status symbols, and patrilineal demands for heirs. Legal in most Muslim-majority countries (with exceptions such as Turkey and Tunisia where it is banned); usage rates are relatively low (often <1% among general population).
South Asia (India, Nepal) Polygyny among Hindu royalty and certain Muslim communities; fraternal polyandry in Himalayan regions. For polygyny: royal alliances, maintenance of male lineage, and cultural/religious norms. For polyandry: scarcity of arable land to prevent inheritance division and preserve family estates. In India, polygamy is illegal for Hindus (since 1955) but remains legal for Muslims under personal law. Nepal and Bhutan have outlawed polygamy; polyandry is not legally recognized.
East Asia (China, Korea, Japan) De facto polygyny through concubinage historically; one official wife with additional concubines common among elites. Driven by Confucian values emphasizing male heirs, social status, and agrarian wealth; a symbol of elite privilege. All East Asian states now enforce strict monogamy; plural marriage is criminalized (e.g., bigamy laws) despite occasional unofficial mistress arrangements.
Southeast Asia (Thailand, Malaysia, Indonesia) Traditional polygyny across various classes; in Thailand (pre-1935 "mia noi" system) and Islamic polygyny in Malay/Indonesian communities. A blend of indigenous traditions and Islamic norms; driven by economic capacity and social prestige. In some cases, the concept of a “minor wife” persists culturally. Legal systems vary: Thailand abolished polygamy in 1935 while it remains legal in Muslim-majority contexts in Malaysia and Indonesia (albeit under regulated conditions). Practice is declining among younger generations.
Oceania (Polynesia, Aboriginal Australia) Polygyny was common among Polynesian chiefs and Indigenous Australian elders. In Polynesia, used for alliance-building and enhancing mana (spiritual prestige); in Aboriginal contexts, to increase resource management and ensure collective child care. Modern legal systems enforce monogamy; historical practices now survive only as cultural memory or informal arrangements.
Indigenous Americas Polygyny practiced by many Native American tribes, often as sororal polygyny; polyandry was virtually non-existent. Economic cooperation (e.g., processing resources), care for widows, and social alliance-building were key. In some Amazonian tribes, the belief in partible paternity allowed for a form of multi-fatherhood. Polygamy is outlawed in modern U.S. and Canadian law; traditional practices persist only as historical memory or in isolated instances.
Mormons (LDS, USA) Polygyny was once widely practiced (affecting approximately 20–30% of families in 19th-century Utah) as “plural marriage.” Driven by religious doctrines (a restoration of Old Testament practices), demographic needs on the frontier, and communal pressures for rapid population growth. Officially banned by the LDS Church in 1890 (with subsequent legal prohibitions in the U.S.); now only practiced secretly by fundamentalist groups.
