How Atwood Remixes Real-World Plural Marriage

Gilead’s domestic triad—Wife, Handmaid, Martha—mirrors countless historical polygynous models that stratify female partners by status. Below, key quotations are paired with ethnographic parallels.

“We are for breeding purposes; we aren’t concubines … We are two-legged wombs, that’s all: sacred vessels, ambulatory chalices.” Offred, Ch 17 (Household)
“Blessed be the meek.” Aunt Lydia, Ch 15 (Household / Ceremony)
“Behold my maid Bilhah. She shall bear upon my knees, that I may also have children by her.” Commander reading Genesis 30:3, Ch 15 (Household)

Parallels Expanded

  • Weaponised Fertility: Like Yoruba kings who took second wives when the first was barren, Commander Waterford invokes Genesis to justify reproductive conscription.
  • Rank-Based Dress Codes: Handmaids’ red recalls the Ottoman harem’s colour hierarchy (concubines in crimson, valide sultan in royal blue).
  • Domestic Espionage: Zulu co-wives traditionally reported each other’s infractions to the husband; likewise Rita and Cora surveil Offred for Serena.
  • State-Service Marriage: Mao-era China assigned model workers a “model spouse.” Gilead likewise distributes Handmaids as state resources.
  • Surplus Bachelors & Militarisation: Maasai warriors delay marriage until elder age; Gilead’s Guardians are kept single, funneling frustration into loyalty.
  • Scriptural Legitimacy: Medieval Jewish polygyny cited Kings I; Gilead cites Rachel/Bilhah—both cherry-pick scripture while omitting caveats.
  • Spatial Choreography: Qing concubines were confined to separate courtyards; Handmaids are locked to their rooms, allowed out only for Ceremony or shopping.
  • Birth Pageantry: Among Zulu, co-wives ululate for a labouring wife—echoed in the choreographed chanting around Janine’s delivery.
  • Name Erasure: Concubines in imperial China lost surnames, gaining the rank “First Consort,” mirroring Offred’s patronymic (Of-Fred).

Additional Textual Anchors

The novel supplies dozens of micro-scenes that echo ethnographic detail:

  • Ch 14 – Wives sit “like a queen on a throne” during birth: identical to Hausa polygynous seclusion rites (kulle).
  • Ch 28 – Offred recalls magazines showing “women in bikinis”: Atwood contrasts sexual display (concubine markets) with enforced modesty (Gilead).
  • Ch 31 – Serena’s cigarette negotiation (“Maybe… another child?”) mirrors bargaining recorded by Evans-Pritchard between senior and junior Nuer wives.
