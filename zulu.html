Geographic Location & Time Period

Centered in KwaZulu-Natal, the Zulu Kingdom flourished in the late 18th–19th centuries. Polygyny continued into modern times under customary law.

Cultural Traditions & Beliefs

Lobola (bridewealth in cattle) formalizes marriages. Chiefs and successful farmers traditionally took multiple wives to cement political alliances and ensure numerous heirs.

Sociopolitical Structure

Zulu homesteads are organized under a patriarch and his wives. The first wife (inhlunkulu) holds ceremonial seniority; co-wives manage distinct tasks like fieldwork, beer brewing, and childcare.

  • Alliances: Each marriage connects clans, expanding influence.
  • Legacy: More wives ensured plenty of sons for lineage continuity.
  • Labor Division: Co-wives share agricultural and domestic duties.

Anthropological Analysis

Ethnographic accounts reveal that while co-wives often support each other, jealousy over status and resource allocation was a recurring theme—captured in the proverb, “A single wife cannot feed her children alone.”

Legal & Modern Status

The Recognition of Customary Marriages Act (1998) legalizes polygyny under strict conditions: all spouses must be registered and treated equally. Urban Zulus increasingly favor monogamy, but rural chiefs still maintain multiple-wife households.

Textual Evidence

“Umfazi ongashadanga akayena umfazi.”
— “A woman not married is not considered a woman.”
