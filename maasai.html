Geographic Location & Time Period

The Maasai roam the semi-arid plains of southern Kenya and northern Tanzania. Polygyny has been idealized for centuries among elder men as part of their age-set social system.

Cultural Traditions & Beliefs

Marriage is arranged by elders, with bridewealth paid in cattle. Each wife resides in her own enkang (hut), where she rears children, milks cows, and tends the household. Cattle not only measure wealth but also determine how many wives a man may afford.

Sociopolitical Structure

Maasai society is patriarchal and gerontocratic: elders hold decision-making power. Polygyny extends a man’s labor force for herding and cultivates alliances between clans. Senior wives coordinate junior wives, ensuring order in the homestead.

  • Pastoral Productivity: More wives mean more labor for cattle management.
  • Age-Set Alliances: Marriages reinforce bonds across warrior and elder classes.
  • Widow Support: Polygyny provides security for women who lose husbands early due to age gaps.

Anthropological Analysis

Ethnographers note that while cooperation among co-wives is common during milking or child care, competition arises when inheritance becomes pressing. The proverb “A man without cattle has no bride” encapsulates the economic logic driving polygyny.

Legal & Modern Status

Customary law in Kenya and Tanzania permits polygynous unions. However, urbanization, formal education, and alternative livelihoods are gradually reducing its incidence, especially among younger generations.

Textual Evidence

“A man with many cows may marry many wives; a poor man stays alone.”
— Maasai Proverb
