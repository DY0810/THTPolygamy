Historical Context

From 7th century CE onward, Islamic law (Quran 4:3) allowed up to four wives if the husband treats them equitably. Early Muslim empires institutionalized regulated polygyny.

Cultural Traditions & Beliefs

Elite households (e.g., Ottoman sultans) maintained large harems. Rural polygyny served to care for widows and orphans after wars.

Sociopolitical Structure

Men petitioned Sharia courts for additional wives; some modern laws require first-wife consent. Women retained dowry rights and separate living quarters in wealthy homes.

  • Elite Display: Polygyny signaled wealth and political power.
  • Social Safety: Provided for vulnerable women in war-torn communities.
  • Legal Checks: Modern reforms impose financial and consent conditions.

Anthropological Analysis

Studies highlight the difficulty of equality among co-wives, leading to calls for stricter regulation or abolition. Survey data show under 5% prevalence in most countries today.

Legal & Modern Status

Polygyny remains legal in many Muslim-majority states (Saudi Arabia, UAE) but banned in Turkey, Tunisia. Court permissions and consent laws vary widely.

Textual Evidence

“Marry women of your choice, two, three, or four; if you fear injustice, then only one.”
— Quran 4:3
