Geographic & Environmental Context

In Tibet, Nepal, Ladakh and surrounding highlands, extreme altitude and limited arable land drove the practice of fraternal polyandry into the 20th century.

Cultural Traditions & Beliefs

Brothers collectively marry one bride, pooling inheritance and ensuring one household remains intact. Legends invoke the Pandava brothers and Draupadi as cultural precedent.

Sociopolitical Structure

Polyandry kept land undivided across generations, maintained family labor units for herding yak and barley cultivation, and curbed population growth to match scarce resources.

  • Impartible Inheritance: Land remains under one ownership.
  • Labor Consolidation: Multiple husbands share agricultural tasks.
  • Population Control: Collective marriage limits total children.

Anthropological Analysis

Research shows higher per-capita wealth and food security in polyandrous families than in hypothetical split households. Cooperation among brothers is critical to success.

Legal & Modern Status

Banned by Chinese Marriage Law (1950) and South Asian codes; practice endures informally in remote valleys, often unregistered.

Textual Evidence

“We share one bride so that our father’s land may survive.”
— Himalayan Oral Tradition
